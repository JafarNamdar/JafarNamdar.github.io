Research

My current research is divided in two major categories. First, I use observational data to analyze and understand the vulnerability of global supply networks against disruptions. For this purpose, I collect data from Bloomberg, WRDS, FactSets and EIKON as well as unstructured data sources from the web and social media with predictive analytics, text mining, and network science. I also use optimization techniques to design supply networks that are efficient, yet resilient during disruptions.

    Applied Optimization

  • Namdar, J., Torabi, S. A., Sahebjamnia, N., & Nilkanth Pradhan, N. (2020). “Business continuity-inspired resilient supply chain network design.” International Journal of Production Research, 1-37. link
    • Supply chains are prone to several operational and disruption risks. In order to design a resilient supply chain network capable of responding to such potential risks suitably, this paper proposes a novel framework for the business continuity-inspired resilient supply chain network design (BCRSCND) problem, which includes three steps. First, four resilience dimensions including Anticipation, Preparation, Robustness, and Recovery are considered to quantify the resilience score of each facility using a multi-criteria decision-making technique and considering a comprehensive set of resilience strategies. In the second step, the critical processes and their business continuity metrics (which are vital for supply chain continuity), are identified. The outputs of the first two steps provide the inputs of a novel two-stage mixed possibilistic-stochastic programing (TSMPSP) model. The model aims to design a multi-echelon, multi-product resilient supply chain network under both operational and disruption risks. The proposed TSMPSP model allows decision makers to incorporate their risk attitudes into the design process. After converting the original TSMPSP model into the crisp counterpart, several sensitivity analyses are conducted on different features of hypothetical disruptions (i.e. their severity, likelihood and location) and DM’s risk attitudes from which useful managerial insights are provided.
  • Keywords: Resilient Management, Supply Chain Design, Business Continuity Management, Two-Stage Stochastic Programming, Pssibilistic Programming

  • Namdar, J., Li, X., Sawhney, R., & Pradhan, N. (2018). “Supply chain resilience for single and multiple sourcing in the presence of disruption risks.” International Journal of Production Research, 56(6), 2339-2360. link [in the list of top cited papers in 2018]
    • This paper investigates the use of sourcing strategies to achieve supply chain resilience under disruptions. The coping strategies considered are single and multiple sourcing, backup supplier contracts, spot purchasing, and collaboration and visibility. Collaboration and visibility, which affect suppliers’ recovery capabilities and a buyer’s warning capability, have not been similarly modelled in the past. A scenario-based mathematical model is developed such that it considers objectives under uncertainties including disruption risks and operational risks. A broad numerical study examines its output for various risk attitudes in a decision-maker, ranging from risk neutral to risk averse. The sensitivity of procurement strategies to other key parameters such as recovery and warning capabilities is examined. One of the major findings is that buyer’s warning capability plays a vital role in enhancing supply chain resilience. We seek to build on these efforts to further support disruption planning and mitigation and to obtain a deeper understanding of the relationship between supply chain characteristics and resilience.
  • Keywords: Resilient Supply Chain Design, Disruption Risks, Collaboration Two-Stage Stochastic Programming, Mixed-Integer Programming

  • Torabi, S. A., Namdar, J., Hatefi, S. M., & Jolai, F. (2016). “An enhanced possibilistic programming approach for reliable closed-loop supply chain network design.” International Journal of Production Research, 54(5), 1358-1387. link
    • Most of current logistics network design models in the literature typically assume that facilities are always available and absolutely reliable while in practice, they are always subject to several operational and disruption risks. This paper proposes a reliable closed-loop supply chain network design model, which accounts for both partial and complete facility disruptions as well as the uncertainty in the critical input data. The proposed model is of mixed integer possibilistic linear programming type that aims to minimise simultaneously the total cost of opening new facilities and the expected cost of disruption scenarios. An enhanced possibilistic programming approach is proposed to deal with the epistemic uncertainty in input data. Furthermore, the p-robustness criterion is used to limit the cost of disruption scenarios and protect the designed network against random facility disruptions. Several numerical experiments along with sensitivity analyses on uncertain parameters are conducted to illustrate the significance and applicability of the developed model as well as the effectiveness of the proposed solution approach. Our results demonstrate that operational and disruption risks considerably affect the whole structure of the designed network and they must be taken into account when designing a reliable closed-loop logistics network.
  • Keywords: Disruptions Risks, Fuzzy Possibilistic Programming, p-Robustness, Miexd-Integer Programming
    •
  • Sahebjamnia, N., Tavakkoli-Moghaddam, R., Namdar, J., & Rezaei Soufi, H. (2016). “Designing a reliable distribution network with facility fortification and transshipment under partial and complete disruptions.” International Journal of Engineering , 29(9), 1273-1281. link
    • This paper designs a reliability distribution network with limited capacity under partial and complete facility disruptions. To increase the reliability of the distribution network, a new mixed integer linear programing model is developed by considering multiple mitigation strategies including diversification, fortification, and transshipment. The distribution network constitutes of reliable distribution centers are more expensive, always available and not affected by disruption, and unreliable distribution centers. Thus, they might be fortified at any level of reliability. Several numerical examples with sensitivity analyses are conducted to illustrate the usefulness of the proposed model. Results demonstrate that the transshipment strategy is more effective than the other mitigation strategies on distribution network reliability and cost.
  • Keywords: Distribution Network Design, Disruption Risks, Mitigation Strategies, Pssibilistic Programming

    Applied Econometrics

  • Namdar, J., Pant, G., Blackhurst, J. “Impact of Industrial and Geopolitical Concentration of Upstream Industries on Firm Resilience.” Journal of Operations Management (Preparing for 2nd Round Review; Reject-and-Resubmit)
    • The concentration of industries can provide benefits of efficiency and scale. However, the dark side can lead to exposing the downstream firms to vulnerabilities during disruptions. In addition to the well-known notion of concentration due to the dominance of a few firms within an industry (i.e., industrial concentration), our study highlights the role of a relatively under-recognized dimension of geopolitical concentration. Using a large data set of firms, supply chain configurations, and key firm performance metrics, we find that high industrial and geopolitical concentration in a firm’s upstream industries has a significant negative effect on the firm’s resilience during an external disruption. Our findings are based on the globally disruptive shock faced by firms in 2020 due to the COVID-19 pandemic. In particular, the sales growth of firms whose suppliers are operating in high concentrated industries saw a relative drop of almost 5 percentage points during the pandemic period. The negative effect is statistically and economically significant and is consistently identified through three different empirical strategies. Our robust findings provide insights for both policy makers and managers for monitoring and mitigating the risks that industrial and geopolitical concentration impose on downstream firms’ resilience.
  • Keywords: Global Supply Chain Network, Firms Resilience, Industrial & Geopolitical Concentrations, COVID-19

  • Namdar, J., Zhao, K., Blackhurst, J. “Supply Chain Risk Management: A Guide to Disruption Resilience through Effective Resilience Strategy Choice” Journal of Business Logistics (Preparing for 2nd Round Review)
    • How do supply chain managers know which risk mitigation or resilience strategies are best in a given disruption scenario? In this paper, we demonstrate how different disruption attributes impact supply chain performance through both direct and indirect interaction. Next, we develop an empirically grounded simulation to determine the specific resilience strategies that are most effective against disruptions in various categories for improving supply chain performance after a disruption. These results are then used to develop a guide for supply chain risk management strategy selection. We expand on prior work by defining disruptions based on varying levels and categories of five attributes (disruption type, location, severity, likelihood, and length). This allows us to explain and resolve the equivocal implications regarding the relationship between disruption attributes and supply chain performance and the interaction of these attributes. Our findings can help managers develop an effective resilience strategy based on the attributes of a specific disruption based on type, location, severity, likelihood and length.
  • Keywords: Supply Chain Disruption, Resilience, Disruption Mitigation, Simulation
    •
  • Namdar, J., Blackhurst, J., Azadegan, A. “On synergistic effects of resilience strategies: Developing a layered defense approach.” International Journal of Production Research (Under 2nd Round Review)
    • Supply chain literature has amply explored the effect of different resilience strategies in the face of supply chain disruptions. Firms often apply a multitude of resilience strategies in tandem. Such strategies can vary from slack inventory to volume flexibility and responsiveness to backup capacity. Yet, there is a lack of empirical or analytical evidence in how the combination of resilience strategies affect firm capabilities in the face of supply chain disruptions. In this paper, we use simulation modeling techniques to determine the effect of different combinations of resilience strategies in a systematic and stepwise manner. Our modeling considers different disruption attributes (capacity or delay), their effect (severity and likelihood), and their origin (i.e., upstream or downstream). A number of interesting observations are made. First, combining resilience strategies is not always beneficial and can occasionally have detrimental effects. Moreover, resilience strategies that are beneficial at the node (firm) level may prove ineffective at the system level. We also find that inventory and volume flexibility are strategies that combine well with others. The study offers several contributions to research and management in supply chain disruption and the study of resilience.
  • Keywords: Supply Chain Resilience, Supply Chain Disruption, Propagation, Layered Defense Theory
    •
  • Namdar, J., Suyong, S., Blackhurst, J., Zhao, K. “Predicting Nexus Suppliers to Prevent Cascading Supply Chain Disruptions.” Journal of Business Logistics (Under 1st Round Review)
    • Supply networks are vulnerable to disruptions not only because of their immediate damages but also because of their cascading effects. The structure of supply networks affects and may inadvertently accelerate the propagation of disruptions. Yet, from a systematic view, little is known about the link between the structural characteristics of supply networks and the disruption cascades. In the first part of this study, we examine the effect of three network structural characteristics: modularity, assortativity, and structural holes. We find that a lack of structural holes in a supplier’s ego network decreases the impact of a cascade emanating from that supplier. Moreover, quantile regression reveals that modularity and assortativity have heterogenous effects on cascading disruptions across the conditional distributions of the outcome variables of interest. Modularity and assortativity generally have deteriorating effects on network resilience; however, they have beneficial effects during extreme events. In the second part of this study, we develop a new predictive model to identify the nexus suppliers whose disruptions would have a large impact on focal firms’ operations.
  • Keywords: Supply Network, Nexus Supplier, Agent-Based Simulation, Quantile Regression, Multilevel Model
